
## Introduction

One of the **most important applications of differential calculus** is **optimization**, where we find the best way to achieve a goal. Optimization problems often involve:

- Minimizing costs or materials (e.g., **designing a can that uses the least material**)
- Maximizing efficiency (e.g., **maximum acceleration a spacecraft can achieve**)
- Biological optimization (e.g., **airflow in windpipes** or **branching angles in blood vessels**)

Mathematically, **these problems reduce to finding maximum or minimum values of a function**.

---

## 1. Absolute and Local Extreme Values

### Absolute (Global) Maximum and Minimum

- Let $f$ be a function defined on a domain $D$.
- Let $c \in D$.  

**Definition:**  

- $f(c)$ is the **absolute maximum** if:  
  $$
  f(c) \ge f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in D
  $$
- $f(c)$ is the **absolute minimum** if:  
  $$
  f(c) \le f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in D
  $$

- Absolute extreme values are also called **global extrema**.  
- The **highest point** on the graph represents the **absolute maximum**, the **lowest point** represents the **absolute minimum**.

**Example:**  
For a function $f(x)$ defined on an interval, if:

- $f(3) = 5$ → absolute maximum  
- $f(6) = -2$ → absolute minimum  

Then 5 is the highest value overall, and -2 is the lowest value overall.

### Local (Relative) Maximum and Minimum

- Sometimes we focus **only on values near a point**.  

**Definition:**  

- $f(c)$ is a **local maximum** if $f(c) \ge f(x)$ for all $x$ near $c$.
- $f(c)$ is a **local minimum** if $f(c) \le f(x)$ for all $x$ near $c$.

> "Near $c$" means within some **open interval** containing $c$.  
> Local extrema **cannot occur at endpoints** of the domain.

**Observation:**  
- A function can have **local extrema that are not absolute extrema**.  
- Conversely, absolute extrema may occur **at endpoints**.

**Example Graphs:**

- Local maximum at $x = b$ (highest in a small neighborhood)  
- Local minimum at $x = e$ (lowest in a small neighborhood)  
- Absolute maximum at $x = d$, absolute minimum at $x = a$

### Examples

1. $f(x) = -3x^4 + 16x^3 + 18x^2$ on $[-1,4]$  

- Local maximum: $f(1) = -5$  
- Absolute maximum: $f(-2) = 37$ (at endpoint)  
- Local minimum: $f(0) = 0$  
- Absolute minimum: $f(3) = -227$

2. $f(x) = \cos x$  

- Absolute and local maxima: $f(2n\pi) = 1$ (for all integers $n$)  
- Absolute and local minima: $f((2n+1)\pi) = -1$

3. $f(x) = x^2$  

- Absolute minimum: $f(0) = 0$  
- No absolute maximum (parabola opens upward)  

4. $f(x) = x^3$  

- No local or absolute extrema (graph keeps increasing/decreasing)

## 2. The Extreme Value Theorem

**Theorem:**  
If $f$ is **continuous** on a **closed interval** $[a, b]$, then $f$ attains:

- An **absolute maximum** $f(c)$ at some $c \in [a, b]$  
- An **absolute minimum** $f(d)$ at some $d \in [a, b]$  

**Key Notes:**

- If $f$ is not continuous, extreme values may not exist.  
- If the interval is **not closed**, extreme values may not exist.  

**Example:**  

- A continuous function on $[0, 2]$ always attains extreme values.  
- A continuous function on $(0, 2)$ may **not attain** maxima or minima.

## 3. Critical Numbers

To find **local extrema**, we examine points where the tangent is horizontal or the derivative does not exist.

**Definition:**  

- A number $c$ in the domain of $f$ is a **critical number** if:  
  $$
  f'(c) = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad f'(c) \text{ does not exist}
  $$

**Fermat’s Theorem:**  

- If $f$ has a **local maximum or minimum** at $c$ and $f'(c)$ exists, then:  
  $$
  f'(c) = 0
  $$

**Caution:**  

- $f'(c) = 0$ **does not guarantee** a local extremum.  
- An extremum may exist where $f'(c)$ does not exist (e.g., $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$).

**Examples:**

1. $f(x) = x^3$ → $f'(0) = 0$, but no extremum  
2. $f(x) = |x|$ → extremum at $x=0$, but $f'(0)$ does not exist

## 4. Finding Absolute Extrema: The Closed Interval Method

For a **continuous function** $f$ on a **closed interval** $[a, b]$:

1. Compute **critical numbers** of $f$ in $(a, b)$  
2. Evaluate $f$ at all critical numbers **and endpoints** $a$ and $b$  
3. **Absolute maximum** = largest value among these  
   **Absolute minimum** = smallest value among these  

**Step-by-Step Example:**

- Function: $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 1$ on $[1/2, 4]$

1. Derivative: $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x = 3x(x - 2)$  
2. Critical numbers: $x = 0, 2$ → only $x = 2$ in $[1/2,4]$  
3. Values:  
   $$
   f(2) = 2^3 - 3(2^2) + 1 = 8 - 12 + 1 = -3
   $$  
   $$
   f(1/2) = (1/2)^3 - 3(1/2)^2 + 1 = 1/8 - 3/4 + 1 = 3/8
   $$  
   $$
   f(4) = 64 - 48 + 1 = 17
   $$

- Absolute maximum = $f(4) = 17$ (at endpoint)  
- Absolute minimum = $f(2) = -3$ (at critical number)

## 5. Applications to Real Problems

**Example 1: Space Shuttle Acceleration**  

- Velocity: $v(t) = 0.000397t^3 - 0.02752t^2 + 7.196t + 0.9397$  
- Acceleration: $a(t) = v'(t) = 0.001191t^2 - 0.05504t + 7.196$  

**Closed Interval Method:**

1. Find critical numbers: $a'(t) = 0.002382t - 0.05504 = 0 \implies t \approx 23.12$  
2. Evaluate acceleration:  
   - $a(0) = 7.196$  
   - $a(23.12) \approx 6.56$  
   - $a(126) \approx 19.16$  

- Maximum acceleration = 19.16 m/s² (at $t = 126$)  
- Minimum acceleration = 6.56 m/s² (at $t \approx 23.12$)  

**Interpretation:**  
- Maximum acceleration occurs at the end of the booster phase  
- Important for **astronaut safety and engineering design**

## 6. Summary of Steps for Finding Extreme Values

1. Determine if $f$ is continuous on a **closed interval**  
2. Find **derivative** $f'(x)$  
3. Find **critical numbers**: $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ undefined  
4. Evaluate $f$ at **critical numbers and endpoints**  
5. Compare values to determine **absolute maximum and minimum**  
6. Check for **local extrema** in the interior

**Key Takeaways:**

- Absolute extrema can occur at **critical numbers or endpoints**  
- Local extrema occur at **critical numbers**  
- Fermat’s Theorem provides a **necessary condition**, not sufficient  
- Critical points where derivative does not exist **may be extrema**  
- Always evaluate endpoints for functions on closed intervals
